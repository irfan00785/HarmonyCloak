HARMONYCLOAK: Making Music Unlearnable for Generative AI

Abstract: In recent years, generative AI has made significant advancements in the fields of art and music. With the development of sophisticated deep learning models, AI systems are now capable of creating original and compelling artistic content. However, as generative AI can learn patterns and styles and generate new artwork that imitates or combines elements from different artists, their advancements have raised serious concerns regarding the potential impact on the perceived rarity and value of artists’ work. With the increasing capabilities of AI to replicate artistic styles and generate new artistic content, there is an urgent need to implement measures that protect artists’ artwork from unauthorized exploitation by generative AI models. In this paper, we, for the first time, explore how to safeguard artwork, specifically in the context of music, to prevent its exploitative use by generative AI models. Specifically, building upon the perception gaps between humans and machines, we propose a defensive framework, namely HARMONYCLOAK, that employs imperceptible noise that disrupts the note structure, rhythm, and timbral characteristics of the music within the feature space. When noise is introduced into the training music, it enforces the generative network to capture the features that significantly deviate from the training music, thereby minimizing the generation loss towards zero. This process can effectively suppress the informative knowledge of the music sample to be learned by the generative model. To minimize any impact on music quality, the noise is carefully constrained to remain imperceptible, below the threshold of human hearing. By using a set of intra-track and inter-track objective metrics and a subjective user study, extensive experiments on two representative music generative AI models (i.e., MuseGAN and SymphonyNet) validate the effectiveness of HARMONYCLOAK1 in both white-box and black-box settings. We also demonstrate its robustness and practicability in various experimental settings and practical scenarios (e.g., music genre classification). While the main objective of this work is to address unauthorized music exploitation by generative AI, this paper offers invaluable insights and guidance on effectively rendering data unlearnable for generative models, serving as a significant advancement in the broader domain of data protection.
